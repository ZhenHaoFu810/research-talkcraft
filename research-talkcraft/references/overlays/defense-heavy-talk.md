# Overlay: Defense-Heavy Talk

## Audience Profile

- **Primary:** Thesis committee members, qualifying exam committee, or dissertation defense panel.
- **Mixedness:** Usually interdisciplinary. A finance thesis may have an econometrician, a theorist, and an empiricist on the same committee.
- **Motivation:** Evaluating whether the candidate has produced original, rigorous, and scope-appropriate research. The committee is explicitly gatekeeping.

## Assumed Knowledge

The speaker can assume the committee knows:
- The broad literature the thesis contributes to.
- Standard methods in the field.
- Their own prior work if it is relevant.

**Do not over-explain:** The general motivation for the field, standard methods, or well-known datasets.
**Do explain:** Exactly what is original, why the chosen methods were selected over alternatives, and how the chapters connect.

## Likely Challenges

| Challenge | Why It Happens | How To Prepare |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Contribution boundary | The committee wants to know what the candidate did vs. what the advisors or coauthors did. | Use first-person singular for your own contributions. Clearly attribute collaborative work. |
| Methodological justification | Every major method choice must be defensible. | For each method: "We chose X over Y because [reason]. The tradeoff is [cost]." |
| Scope discipline | The committee will ask why obvious extensions were not included. | Proactively draw scope boundaries: "This chapter focuses on X. Extensions to Y and Z are discussed in Chapter N / left for future work." |
| Committee-style attack | One committee member may adopt an adversarial stance to test the candidate's knowledge. | Prepare calm, precise responses. Do not get defensive. It is okay to say "That is a fair concern; we address it in [chapter/appendix]." |
| Internal consistency | Cross-chapter contradictions or conflicting assumptions will be flagged. | Review the entire thesis for consistency before the defense. Flag any intentional differences proactively. |

## Overclaiming Definitions

In defense settings, the following are dangerous overclaiming patterns:

1. **Attributing coauthor work to yourself.**
   - Weak: "I developed the estimation framework."
   - Strong: "I developed the estimation framework. The data collection was led by [Coauthor], and the theoretical model was developed in collaboration with [Coauthor]."

2. **Claiming the thesis solves a problem it only addresses partially.**
   - Weak: "This thesis explains why firms adopt GAI."
   - Strong: "This thesis documents and characterizes GAI adoption in U.S. public-firm financial disclosures. It does not explain the welfare consequences of adoption, which is an important extension."

3. **Casual causal language in a reduced-form chapter.**
   - Weak: "The results show that regulation causes firms to exit."
   - Strong: "The results show a sharp increase in exit rates after the regulatory change. Under the assumption that [identification assumption], this is consistent with a causal effect."

## Pacing and Tone

- **Pacing:** Deliberate. Do not rush through the contribution statement. Spend extra time on the motivation-identification-results arc for each chapter.
- **Tone:** Respectful, confident, and transparent. The candidate is the expert on this specific thesis, but the committee are senior experts on the broader field.
- **Formality:** High. Use full names, precise terminology, and explicit scope language.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Treating the defense like a seminar.** The committee has read the thesis. They want justification, not exposition.
2. **Hiding known limitations.** The committee already knows the limitations. Pretending they do not exist signals immaturity.
3. **Overstating contribution relative to the literature.** The committee will compare the thesis to the prior work they know intimately.
4. **Inconsistent scope claims across chapters.** Chapter 2 may claim to study "all firms" while Chapter 3 uses a subsample. Explain why.
5. **No clear narrative thread across chapters.** A collection of unrelated papers is harder to defend than a coherent thesis.

## Proactive Defense Requirements

Before the committee asks, the script should already address:

1. **What is the single most important contribution of this thesis?** One sentence, stated early and repeated at the end.
2. **What did you do, and what did your coauthors do?** Clear attribution.
3. **Why this method and not the obvious alternative?** One sentence per major method choice.
4. **What are the three biggest limitations?** Name them proactively and explain why they do not invalidate the main results.
5. **How do the chapters connect?** One paragraph that shows the thesis is more than a bundle of papers.
