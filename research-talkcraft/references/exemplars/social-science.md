# Social Science / Mixed-Methods Exemplars

## Exemplar 1: Qualitative-Framing Content

**Scenario:** Mixed-methods workshop  
**Domain:** Political Science / Sociology  
**Audience:** Quantitative and qualitative researchers  
**Input material type:** Methods slide describing qualitative coding

### Slide content summary
- Title: "Qualitative coding process"
- Bullets:
  - 47 interviews with community organizers
  - Open coding followed by axial coding
  - Inter-rater reliability: kappa = 0.82

### Weak script
"We conducted 47 interviews with community organizers. We used open coding followed by axial coding. Inter-rater reliability was 0.82 kappa."

### Critique
- Reads bullets without explaining what open and axial coding mean.
- A quantitative audience may dismiss qualitative coding as "subjective" without understanding the rigor.
- No explanation of why 47 interviews were enough.

### Improved script
"To understand how community organizers make decisions under resource constraints, we interviewed 47 organizers across three cities. The analysis uses a standard qualitative technique called grounded theory coding. The first pass is open coding: we read every transcript and tag any concept that seems relevant — things like 'grant pressure,' 'coalition building,' 'moral exhaustion.' The second pass is axial coding: we group those tags into categories and look for relationships between them. To ensure rigor, two researchers coded every transcript independently, and our agreement rate was 0.82 Cohen's kappa — that is considered strong agreement. So while the data is qualitative, the coding process is systematic and replicable."

### Why the revision works
- Explains open and axial coding in plain language with concrete examples.
- Justifies the sample size implicitly by describing the depth of analysis.
- Addresses the quantitative audience's skepticism by emphasizing inter-rater reliability.
- Frames qualitative methods as systematic, not impressionistic.

## Exemplar 2: Policy-Implication Content

**Scenario:** Stakeholder briefing  
**Domain:** Public Policy / Economics  
**Audience:** Policymakers, practitioners, and a few academics  
**Input material type:** Implications slide

### Slide content summary
- Title: "Policy implications"
- Bullets:
  - Cash transfers reduce child poverty by 12%
  - Effects are largest in rural areas
  - Scale-up would cost $2.1 billion annually

### Weak script
"Our findings have important policy implications. Cash transfers reduce child poverty by 12 percent. The effects are largest in rural areas. Scaling up would cost 2.1 billion dollars annually."

### Critique
- "Important policy implications" is filler.
- Reports numbers without saying what a policymaker should do with them.
- No cost-effectiveness framing.
- No acknowledgment of implementation barriers.

### Improved script
"What does this mean for policy? The program we studied reduces child poverty by 12 percent, and the gains are concentrated in rural areas where service delivery is hardest. At a national scale, that would cost roughly 2.1 billion dollars per year. To put that in context, the current child tax credit costs about 100 billion annually, so this is a relatively small addition for a meaningful reduction in rural poverty. The caveat is that our experiment was run in counties with strong local administration. Scaling to weaker institutional settings may require additional investment in delivery capacity. So the case for scale-up is strongest where local governments are already functional."

### Why the revision works
- Frames the cost relative to an existing program the audience knows.
- Acknowledges the scope condition (strong local administration).
- Gives a conditional recommendation rather than an unconditional mandate.
- Respects the mixed audience by keeping numbers concrete but interpretation clear.
