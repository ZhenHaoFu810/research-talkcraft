# General Research Scenario Playbooks

These playbooks adapt tone, depth, and emphasis across common research presentation settings. They do not change the core workflow; they change how the Skill calibrates explanation depth, script priorities, and common failure modes.

---

## 1. Seminar Presentation

### Audience assumptions
- Mixed knowledge: some experts, some newcomers to the sub-field.
- People are there to learn, not to approve or reject.
- Attention span is moderate; the talk is one of several in a series.

### Script priorities
- Strong motivation: why should this audience care?
- Intuition before detail: methods should be explained conceptually before technically.
- Clear takeaways: what should the audience remember tomorrow?
- Generous transitions: help listeners follow the arc without taking notes.

### Common failure modes
- Diving into technical details before establishing relevance.
- Assuming everyone knows the motivating literature.
- Running over time because the speaker tries to cover too much.

### Tone guidance
- Conversational but precise.
- Enthusiastic about the problem, measured about the results.
- Use analogies and examples liberally.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "Why this problem and not the adjacent one?"
- "Can you explain the intuition behind the method?"
- "How does this relate to [well-known work]?"

## 2. Lab or Group Meeting

### Audience assumptions
- Familiar with the group's prior work and shared methods.
- Interested in process, obstacles, and honest progress assessment.
- Less formal; interruptions and discussion are expected.

### Script priorities
- Honest status update: what worked, what didn't, what's next.
- Technical depth is welcome, but only where it affects decisions.
- Clear ask: feedback, help, or a decision.
- Short background; long on new findings and open questions.

### Common failure modes
- Over-explaining basics the group already knows.
- Hiding problems or presenting only polished results.
- No clear ask at the end.

### Tone guidance
- Direct and collaborative.
- It is okay to say "this didn't work" or "we're stuck here."
- Less performative, more conversational.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "Have you tried [obvious alternative]?"
- "Why are you still doing X when Y is clearly better?"
- "Can you show the raw data / the failed run?"

## 3. Conference Talk

### Audience assumptions
- Broad field exposure, but shallow depth in any one sub-topic.
- Attention is fragmented; the talk competes with notifications and hallway conversations.
- Listeners decide quickly whether to stay mentally engaged.

### Script priorities
- Hook in the first 30 seconds.
- One clear message, not three.
- High signal-to-noise: every sentence should earn its place.
- Strong visual-slide integration: the script should complement, not duplicate, what is on screen.

### Common failure modes
- Trying to cover the whole paper in 15 minutes.
- Opening with a slow literature review.
- Overloading the audience with results.
- Weak closing that fizzles instead of reinforcing the message.

### Tone guidance
- Energetic and confident.
- Use memorable phrasing and clear contrasts.
- Frame results as answers to a well-posed question.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "How does this compare to the concurrent work by [Author]?"
- "Can this scale to [real-world setting]?"
- "Isn't this just a special case of [known framework]?"

## 4. Thesis-Defense-Style Presentation

### Audience assumptions
- Committee members have read the thesis or related chapters.
- They are explicitly evaluating rigor, originality, and scope.
- The defense is a formality in some systems and a real gate in others, so the speaker should be prepared for both.

### Script priorities
- Defensible claims: every major statement must be backed by evidence in the thesis.
- Explicit scope boundaries: what is in the thesis, what is not.
- Honest limitations: pretending there are none is worse than admitting them.
- Methodological justification: why this approach was chosen over alternatives.

### Common failure modes
- Treating the defense like a seminar (too casual, not defensive enough).
- Overstating the contribution.
- Hiding limitations the committee already knows about.
- Running long because the speaker tries to cover every chapter.

### Tone guidance
- Respectful but confident.
- It is okay to say "that is outside the scope of this thesis" if it is true.
- Prepare to justify every major methodological choice.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "What is the single most important contribution, and why is it non-obvious?"
- "How do you respond to [specific theoretical or empirical concern]?"
- "Why didn't you address [obvious extension or robustness check]?"

## 5. Mixed-Background Research Update

### Audience assumptions
- Some listeners are technical peers; others are stakeholders, managers, or collaborators from other fields.
- The audience cares about impact and implications more than method details.
- Listeners may not share terminology or assumptions.

### Script priorities
- Dual-level explanation: a high-level takeaway for non-experts, with optional depth for experts.
- Framing in terms of outcomes, not just methods.
- Minimal jargon; when jargon is necessary, define it inline.
- Explicit connection to the broader project or organizational goal.

### Common failure modes
- Speaking only to the experts in the room.
- Using undefined field-specific shorthand.
- Focusing on process rather than outcome.
- No clear "so what" for the non-technical half of the audience.

### Tone guidance
- Inclusive and patient.
- Check in with the audience: "For those less familiar with X, the key idea is..."
- Use concrete examples from the application domain.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "Can you say that without the jargon?"
- "What does this mean for [practical application]?"
- "Why should we fund / continue this line of work?"

## Using the Playbooks

1. **Match the user's stated scenario** to the closest playbook.
2. **Apply the tone and depth calibration** to the Presentation Brief and script.
3. **Prioritize the common failure modes** in the critique stage.
4. **Generate Q&A** from the typical challenge patterns.
5. **If the scenario is unclear**, default to the Seminar Presentation playbook as the safest general case.
6. **If a discipline-specific overlay is active**, use the overlay's audience assumptions and challenge patterns to sharpen the playbook calibration. The overlay does not replace the playbook; it specializes it.
## 6. Chinese Academic Seminar

### Audience assumptions
- Mixed knowledge: faculty, graduate students, and occasional visitors from adjacent departments.
- Audience expects a clear problem statement early; patience for long literature reviews is lower than in some Western seminar cultures.
- Q&A may be delayed until the end, but engaged listeners will signal confusion or agreement non-verbally.

### Script priorities
- State the contribution or main finding within the first 90 seconds. Chinese academic audiences often expect early orientation.
- Use concrete examples rather than abstract framing; "举个例子" is a powerful spoken bridge.
- Keep background concise unless the sub-field is genuinely obscure to this department.
- Transitions should sound spoken, not like section headers from a written paper.

### Common failure modes
- Opening with a slow literature review before stating what the talk is about.
- Overusing written-formal phrases ("本文"、"研究表明"、"具有重要的理论和实践意义") that sound stiff in speech.
- Reading bullets in parallel grammatical structures, creating a mechanical rhythm.
- Weak contribution framing: "填补了研究空白" without specifying what空白, why it matters, or who cares.

### Tone guidance
- Respectful but not overly deferential. It is okay to say "我们发现" rather than "本文发现."
- Use natural spoken Chinese节奏. Replace "基于以上分析" with "所以回过头来看" or "这意味着."
- When challenging prior work, frame it as a puzzle or gap rather than a direct attack.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "你的结果和某某的结论不一致，你怎么解释？"
- "这个机制在中文语境下是否适用？"
- "你的样本选择会不会有内生性问题？"

## 7. Chinese Lab or Group Meeting

### Audience assumptions
- Familiar with the group's shared methods and prior work.
- Expect honest progress assessment, including failed experiments and dead ends.
- Interruptions are common and welcome; the meeting is collaborative, not performative.

### Script priorities
- Lead with the most important result or obstacle, not a long background recap.
- Be direct about what did not work. Phrases like "这个方向试了，但效果不理想" are perfectly acceptable.
- When presenting methods, explain design choices, not just configurations.
- End with a clear ask: feedback, help, or a decision.

### Common failure modes
- Over-explaining basics the group already knows.
- Hiding negative results or framing them as "inconclusive" when they are clearly failures.
- No clear next step or ask at the end.
- Using overly formal written Chinese ("笔者"、" aforementioned") in an informal meeting.

### Tone guidance
- Casual and collaborative. Use spoken contractions and natural fillers ("咱们"、"其实"、"说白了").
- It is okay to say "我也不确定" or "这个需要再想想."
- Less performative, more conversational than a seminar.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "你试过用另一种参数吗？"
- "这个结果是不是因为数据预处理的问题？"
- "下一步打算怎么验证？"

## 8. Chinese Defense-Style Presentation

### Audience assumptions
- Committee members have read the thesis or related chapters.
- The defense is often a formality, but in some institutions it is a real gate — the speaker should be prepared for both.
- Audience expects rigorous scope discipline and explicit boundary-setting.

### Script priorities
- Defensible claims: every major statement must be backed by evidence in the thesis.
- Explicit scope boundaries: what is in the thesis, what is not, and why.
- Honest limitations: pretending there are none is worse than admitting them, especially in front of a committee.
- Methodological justification: why this approach was chosen over alternatives.

### Common failure modes
- Treating the defense like a seminar (too casual, not defensive enough).
- Overstating the contribution with phrases like "首次发现" or "全新的理论框架" when the evidence supports a more modest claim.
- Hiding limitations the committee already knows about.
- Running long because the speaker tries to cover every chapter in equal depth.

### Tone guidance
- Respectful but confident. Use "我们认为" rather than "笔者拙见."
- It is okay to say "这超出了本文的范围，但我可以简要说明思路" if the question is beyond scope.
- Prepare to justify every major methodological choice, but do not apologize for reasonable constraints.

### Typical challenge patterns
- "你最重要的贡献是什么，为什么它不是显而易见的？"
- "你如何回应某某对你的识别策略的质疑？"
- "为什么你没有做某某稳健性检验？"
- "这个结论在中文情境下的外部效度如何？"
